    Solution — Problem 4: Building Complex Statements from Simple Ones

    Problem Recap

    We consider an experiment of two die rolls with the 6×6 grid representation. We define three basic events:

    • \(A\): the sum of the two results is equal to 7
    • \(B\): the first die shows a greater number than the second
    • \(C\): at least one die shows 6

    All answers are presented graphically using ASCII tables.

    Part A — Basic Statements

    Event \(A\): The sum is 7

    We need \((i,j)\) with \(i + j = 7\), i.e., \(j = 7 - i\).

          1 2 3 4 5 6
1     . . . . . X
2     . . . . X .
3     . . . X . .
4     . . X . . .
5     . X . . . .
6     X . . . . .

    \(A = \{(1,6),(2,5),(3,4),(4,3),(5,2),(6,1)\}\), \(|A| = 6\).

    The event forms an anti-diagonal from top-right to bottom-left.

    Event \(B\): The first die is greater than the second

    We need \((i,j)\) with \(i > j\) — all cells strictly below the main diagonal.

          1 2 3 4 5 6
1     . . . . . .
2     X . . . . .
3     X X . . . .
4     X X X . . .
5     X X X X . .
6     X X X X X .

    \(B = \{(i,j) : i > j\}\), \(|B| = 15\).

    The event is the lower triangle below the diagonal.

    Event \(C\): At least one die shows 6

    We need \((i,j)\) where \(i = 6\) or \(j = 6\).

          1 2 3 4 5 6
1     . . . . . X
2     . . . . . X
3     . . . . . X
4     . . . . . X
5     . . . . . X
6     X X X X X X

    \(C = \{(i,j) : i = 6 \text{ or } j = 6\}\), \(|C| = 11\).

    The event is the union of row 6 and column 6 (an L-shape in the bottom-right).

    Part B — Compound Statements

    1. \(A \cup C\): The sum is 7 or at least one die shows 6

    This is the union of \(A\) and \(C\). A cell is marked if it belongs to \(A\) or \(C\) (or both).

          1 2 3 4 5 6
1     . . . . . X    ← (1,6) ∈ A ∩ C
2     . . . . X X    ← (2,5) ∈ A; (2,6) ∈ C
3     . . . X . X    ← (3,4) ∈ A; (3,6) ∈ C
4     . . X . . X    ← (4,3) ∈ A; (4,6) ∈ C
5     . X . . . X    ← (5,2) ∈ A; (5,6) ∈ C
6     X X X X X X    ← row 6 ∈ C; (6,1) ∈ A ∩ C

    \(|A \cup C| = |A| + |C| - |A \cap C| = 6 + 11 - 2 = 15\).

    (The overlapping cells are \((1,6)\) and \((6,1)\), which satisfy both conditions.)

    2. \(A \cap C\): The sum is 7 and at least one die shows 6

    A cell must be in both \(A\) and \(C\): the sum is 7 and at least one die is 6.

    If \(i + j = 7\) and (\(i = 6\) or \(j = 6\)): - \(i = 6 \Rightarrow j = 1\): \((6,1)\) ✅ - \(j = 6 \Rightarrow i = 1\): \((1,6)\) ✅

          1 2 3 4 5 6
1     . . . . . X
2     . . . . . .
3     . . . . . .
4     . . . . . .
5     . . . . . .
6     X . . . . .

    \(A \cap C = \{(1,6),(6,1)\}\), \(|A \cap C| = 2\).

    3. \(B \cap C\): The first die is greater than the second and at least one die shows 6

    We need \(i > j\) and (\(i = 6\) or \(j = 6\)). Since \(i > j\), having \(j = 6\) would require \(i > 6\), which is impossible. So we must have \(i = 6\) and \(j < 6\).

          1 2 3 4 5 6
1     . . . . . .
2     . . . . . .
3     . . . . . .
4     . . . . . .
5     . . . . . .
6     X X X X X .

    \(B \cap C = \{(6,1),(6,2),(6,3),(6,4),(6,5)\}\), \(|B \cap C| = 5\).

    4. \(A \cap B^c\): The sum is 7, but the first die is not greater than the second

    We need \(i + j = 7\) and \(i \leq j\).

    From \(A\): \((1,6),(2,5),(3,4),(4,3),(5,2),(6,1)\). Filter by \(i \leq j\): - \((1,6)\): \(1 \leq 6\) ✅ - \((2,5)\): \(2 \leq 5\) ✅ - \((3,4)\): \(3 \leq 4\) ✅ - \((4,3)\): \(4 > 3\) ❌ - \((5,2)\): \(5 > 2\) ❌ - \((6,1)\): \(6 > 1\) ❌

          1 2 3 4 5 6
1     . . . . . X
2     . . . . X .
3     . . . X . .
4     . . . . . .
5     . . . . . .
6     . . . . . .

    \(A \cap B^c = \{(1,6),(2,5),(3,4)\}\), \(|A \cap B^c| = 3\).

    5. \(A \cap C^c\): The sum is 7, and no die shows 6

    We need \(i + j = 7\) and \(i \neq 6\) and \(j \neq 6\).

    From \(A\), exclude \((1,6)\) and \((6,1)\):

          1 2 3 4 5 6
1     . . . . . .
2     . . . . X .
3     . . . X . .
4     . . X . . .
5     . X . . . .
6     . . . . . .

    \(A \cap C^c = \{(2,5),(3,4),(4,3),(5,2)\}\), \(|A \cap C^c| = 4\).

    6. \(C \cap A^c\): At least one die shows 6, but the sum is not 7

    We need (\(i = 6\) or \(j = 6\)) and \(i + j \neq 7\).

    From \(C\), exclude \((1,6)\) and \((6,1)\):

          1 2 3 4 5 6
1     . . . . . .
2     . . . . . X
3     . . . . . X
4     . . . . . X
5     . . . . . X
6     . X X X X X

    \(C \cap A^c = C \setminus A\), \(|C \cap A^c| = 11 - 2 = 9\).

    7. \(A^c \cap B\): The sum is not 7 and the first die is greater than the second

    We need \(i > j\) and \(i + j \neq 7\). Start from \(B\) and remove elements of \(A\).

    From \(B\): the 15 cells below the diagonal. Remove \(A \cap B = \{(4,3),(5,2),(6,1)\}\).

          1 2 3 4 5 6
1     . . . . . .
2     X . . . . .
3     X X . . . .
4     X X . . . .
5     X . X X . .
6     . X X X X .

    \(A^c \cap B = B \setminus A\), \(|A^c \cap B| = 15 - 3 = 12\).

    8. \(B^c \cap C\): The first die is not greater than the second and at least one die shows 6

    We need \(i \leq j\) and (\(i = 6\) or \(j = 6\)).

    • \(j = 6\), \(i \leq 6\): \((1,6),(2,6),(3,6),(4,6),(5,6),(6,6)\) — all 6 cells
    • \(i = 6\), \(j \geq 6\): only \((6,6)\) — already counted
          1 2 3 4 5 6
1     . . . . . X
2     . . . . . X
3     . . . . . X
4     . . . . . X
5     . . . . . X
6     . . . . . X

    \(B^c \cap C = \{(1,6),(2,6),(3,6),(4,6),(5,6),(6,6)\}\), \(|B^c \cap C| = 6\).

    This is exactly the rightmost column — all outcomes where the second die is 6 and the first die is \(\leq\) the second (which is always true when \(j=6\)).

    9. \((A \cup C)^c\): It is not true that (the sum is 7 or at least one die shows 6)

    By De Morgan's law: \((A \cup C)^c = A^c \cap C^c\).

    We need: the sum is NOT 7, AND neither die is 6. So \(i + j \neq 7\) and \(i \leq 5\) and \(j \leq 5\). We take the 5×5 sub-grid (rows 1–5, columns 1–5) and remove cells with sum 7.

    In the 5×5 sub-grid, which cells have \(i+j=7\)? \((2,5),(3,4),(4,3),(5,2)\) — there are 4 such cells.

          1 2 3 4 5 6
1     X X X X X .
2     X X X X . .
3     X X X . X .
4     X X . X X .
5     X . X X X .
6     . . . . . .

    \(|(A \cup C)^c| = 25 - 4 = 21\).

    10. \((A \cap C)^c\): It is not true that (the sum is 7 and at least one die shows 6)

    By De Morgan's law: \((A \cap C)^c = A^c \cup C^c\).

    This is the complement of \(A \cap C = \{(1,6),(6,1)\}\). So we mark everything except \((1,6)\) and \((6,1)\).

          1 2 3 4 5 6
1     X X X X X .
2     X X X X X X
3     X X X X X X
4     X X X X X X
5     X X X X X X
6     . X X X X X

    \(|(A \cap C)^c| = 36 - 2 = 34\).

    Key observations across all 10 compound events:

    • Union (\(\cup\)) corresponds to the logical OR and produces a larger region.
    • Intersection (\(\cap\)) corresponds to the logical AND and produces a smaller region.
    • Complement (\(^c\)) corresponds to NOT and produces the "negative" of the grid.
    • De Morgan's Laws are visually verifiable: the complement of a union equals the intersection of the complements, and vice versa.
    • The grid representation makes these set-theoretic operations geometrically visible, which is a core pedagogical goal of this problem.
