    The Setup

    There are 350 support tickets, each labeled with two properties:

    • Is it technical? → \(T\) (yes) or \(T^c\) (no)
    • Was it solved during first contact? → \(S\) (yes) or \(S^c\) (no)

    The data:

    Solved (\(S\)) Not solved (\(S^c\)) Total
    Technical (\(T\)) 90 60 150
    Non-technical (\(T^c\)) 160 40 200
    Total 250 100 350

    Step 1 — Probabilities of the four regions

    Divide each cell by 350. The logic: "out of 350 tickets, how many fall into this group?"

    \[ P(T \cap S) = \frac{90}{350} \approx 0.2571 \]
    \[ P(T \cap S^c) = \frac{60}{350} \approx 0.1714 \]
    \[ P(T^c \cap S) = \frac{160}{350} \approx 0.4571 \]
    \[ P(T^c \cap S^c) = \frac{40}{350} \approx 0.1143 \]

    Step 2 — Verify they sum to 1

    \[ \frac{90 + 60 + 160 + 40}{350} = \frac{350}{350} = 1 \checkmark \]

    These four regions cover the entire sample space with no overlap, so their probabilities must sum to exactly 1.

    Step 3 — Union probabilities

    \(P(T \cup S)\) — "technical or solved (or both)"

    \[ P(T \cup S) = P(T) + P(S) - P(T \cap S) = \frac{150}{350} + \frac{250}{350} - \frac{90}{350} = \frac{310}{350} \approx 0.8857 \]

    !!! info "Why do we subtract \(P(T \cap S)\)?"

    When we add $P(T)$ and $P(S)$, the 90 tickets in $T \cap S$ get counted twice — once inside $P(T)$ and once inside $P(S)$. Subtracting $P(T \cap S)$ corrects for that double-counting.

    Alternative using the four regions directly — everything except \(T^c \cap S^c\):

    \[ P(T \cup S) = 1 - P(T^c \cap S^c) = 1 - \frac{40}{350} \approx 0.8857 \]

    \(P(T^c \cup S)\) — "non-technical or solved (or both)"

    \[ P(T^c \cup S) = P(T^c) + P(S) - P(T^c \cap S) = \frac{200}{350} + \frac{250}{350} - \frac{160}{350} = \frac{290}{350} \approx 0.8286 \]

    Step 4 — Conditional probabilities

    \(P(S \mid T)\) — "given that the ticket is technical, what is the probability it was solved?"

    \[ P(S \mid T) = \frac{90}{150} = 0.60 \]

    !!! info "Why do we divide by 150 and not 350?"

    The condition "$T$" means we already know the ticket is technical. So we restrict our universe to only those 150 tickets — the other 200 are irrelevant. Out of those 150, exactly 90 were solved. Formally: $P(S \mid T) = \frac{P(T \cap S)}{P(T)} = \frac{90/350}{150/350} = \frac{90}{150}$. The 350s cancel out, which is why we end up dividing by 150.

    \(P(S \mid T^c)\) — "given that the ticket is non-technical, what is the probability it was solved?"

    \[ P(S \mid T^c) = \frac{160}{200} = 0.80 \]

    By the same logic, we restrict to the 200 non-technical tickets and ask how many of those were solved.

    Step 5 — Interpretation

    60% of technical tickets are solved during first contact, compared to 80% of non-technical ones. The gap shows that \(T\) and \(S\) are not independent — being a technical ticket lowers the probability of first-contact resolution.

    !!! info "What would independence look like numerically?"

    If $T$ and $S$ were independent, knowing the ticket type would tell you nothing about whether it gets solved. Formally, independence means:

$$P(S \mid T) = P(S \mid T^c) = P(S)$$

Here $P(S) = \frac{250}{350} \approx 0.714$, but $P(S \mid T) = 0.60$ and $P(S \mid T^c) = 0.80$. Since $0.60 \neq 0.80 \neq 0.714$, the events are dependent — ticket type clearly affects the outcome.
